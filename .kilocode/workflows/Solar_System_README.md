## Overview

The **Solar System** is a gravitationally bound system of the **Sun** and all objects that orbit it. It formed about **4.568 billion years ago** from the gravitational collapse of a dense region within a giant molecular cloud. The Sun, a **G‑type main‑sequence star (G2V)**, contains **99.86 %** of the system's total mass; the remaining mass is distributed among eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and interplanetary dust and gas. [reference:0][reference:1]

The planets, in order from the Sun, are **Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune**. They are divided into two main groups: the small, rocky *terrestrial planets* of the inner Solar System and the large, gas‑ and ice‑rich *giant planets* of the outer Solar System. [reference:2][reference:3]

## Formation and Evolution

### Past: The Nebular Hypothesis

The Solar System began as a fragment of a giant molecular cloud composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, with traces of heavier elements. Gravitational collapse, likely triggered by a nearby supernova, caused the cloud to spin faster (conservation of angular momentum) and flatten into a **protoplanetary disc** about 200 AU across. [reference:20]

Most mass collected at the centre, forming the **protosun**. As the pressure and temperature rose, hydrogen fusion ignited in the core, and the Sun entered the main sequence. Meanwhile, dust and gas in the disc coalesced into planetesimals and then into planets through **accretion**. [reference:21]

- Inside the *frost line*, only refractory materials (metals, silicates) could solidify, giving rise to the small **terrestrial planets**.
- Beyond the frost line, volatile ices were abundant, allowing the **giant planets** to grow massive enough to capture hydrogen and helium atmospheres. [reference:22]

### Present and Future

The Solar System is currently in a relatively stable phase, although its long‑term evolution is **chaotic**. In about 5 billion years, the Sun will exhaust its core hydrogen, swell into a **red giant**, and eventually shed its outer layers to become a **white dwarf**. This transformation will likely destroy Mercury and Venus and render Earth uninhabitable. [reference:23]

## Structure and Regions

Astronomers traditionally divide the Solar System into several distinct zones:

### Inner Solar System

Comprises the four terrestrial planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) and the **asteroid belt** between Mars and Jupiter. Asteroids are mainly rocky bodies, remnants of the early Solar System that never accreted into a planet. [reference:24][reference:25]

### Outer Solar System

Contains the four giant planets – Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune – and their moons and rings. This region also includes **centaurs**, small icy bodies with unstable orbits that cross those of the giant planets. [reference:26]

### Trans‑Neptunian Region

The region beyond Neptune is home to:

- **Kuiper Belt** – a disc of icy bodies extending from about 30 AU to 50 AU. Pluto, Eris, and other dwarf planets reside here.
- **Scattered Disc** – a sparse population of icy objects with highly inclined and eccentric orbits.
- **Oort Cloud** – a hypothetical spherical shell of icy planetesimals at 2 000–200 000 AU, believed to be the source of long‑period comets. [reference:27]

### Heliosphere and Beyond

The Sun's magnetic field creates a bubble in the interstellar medium called the **heliosphere**. Inside it, the solar wind streams outward at 900 000–2 880 000 km/h. The **heliopause**, where the solar wind is stopped by the interstellar medium, is located at about 120 AU. Beyond it lies interstellar space. [reference:28][reference:29]

## The Sun

The Sun is the dominant object in the Solar System:

- **Mass:** 332 900 Earth masses (1.989 × 10³⁰ kg) – 99.86 % of the total.
- **Spectral type:** G2V (effective temperature ~5 772 K).
- **Core:** Nuclear fusion of hydrogen to helium, releasing 3.846 × 10²⁶ W.
- **Photosphere:** The visible surface, which emits most of the light and heat.
- **Solar wind:** A continuous stream of charged particles that shapes the heliosphere and influences space weather. [reference:30]

The Sun is a **Population I star**, rich in elements heavier than hydrogen and helium (“metals”), which facilitated the formation of planets. [reference:31]

## Dwarf Planets

The **International Astronomical Union (IAU)** recognises five dwarf planets:

- **Ceres** (in the asteroid belt)
- **Pluto**
- **Eris**
- **Makemake**
- **Haumea**

Four additional bodies are often considered dwarf planets: Orcus, Quaoar, Gonggong, and Sedna. Dwarf planets are massive enough to be rounded by their own gravity but have not cleared their orbital neighbourhood. [reference:37]

## Other Bodies

### Natural Satellites (Moons)

There are **758 known moons** in the Solar System. The largest (Ganymede, Titan) are larger than Mercury. Most large moons are in **synchronous rotation**, always showing the same face to their planet. [reference:38]

### Asteroids

Asteroids are rocky remnants, mainly found in:
- The **asteroid belt** (between Mars and Jupiter)
- The **Trojan asteroids** (sharing orbits with planets, especially Jupiter)
- **Near‑Earth asteroids** (orbits that cross Earth's path)

### Comets

Comets are icy bodies that originate in the Kuiper Belt or Oort Cloud. When they approach the Sun, the heat causes their ices to sublimate, creating a visible **coma** and **tail**.

### Interplanetary Medium

The space between the planets is not empty. It contains:
- **Solar wind plasma**
- **Interplanetary dust**
- **Cosmic rays**
- **Neutral gas**

This medium extends to the heliopause at ~120 AU. [reference:39]

## Orbits and Dynamics

- All planets orbit the Sun in the same **counter‑clockwise** direction (as viewed from above Earth's north pole) and lie near the **invariable plane** (ecliptic).
- **Kepler's laws** describe the orbital motion to first order, but gravitational perturbations from other bodies cause long‑term chaotic evolution.
- The **angular momentum** of the Solar System is dominated by the planets, especially Jupiter, although the Sun contains most of the mass. [reference:40][reference:41]

## Habitability

The Solar System's **habitable zone** is located around Earth's orbit, where surface liquid water is possible. Key factors for habitability include:

- **Energy from the Sun** (insolation).
- **Planetary magnetic fields** that deflect cosmic rays.
- **The heliosphere**, which shields the system from high‑energy interstellar particles. [reference:42]

Potential subsurface oceans on moons such as Europa, Enceladus, and Titan are also targets for astrobiology. [reference:43]

## Comparison with Extrasolar Systems

Compared to known exoplanetary systems, the Solar System is **unusual** in several ways:

- It lacks **super‑Earths** – planets with masses between Earth and Neptune.
- It has a clear **gap** between the small terrestrial planets and the large gas/ice giants.
- The orbits are **nearly circular** and well‑separated.
- It belongs to the “ordered” class of planetary systems (as opposed to “similar” or “mixed”). [reference:44]

These characteristics have led to hypotheses such as the **Grand Tack** and **Nice model**, which describe planetary migration in the early Solar System. [reference:45]

## How This Project Uses the Data

This document serves as the **reference data set** for the 3D Solar System simulation. All planetary positions, orbital elements, masses, radii, and physical properties are taken from the Wikipedia article and the sources cited therein. The simulation:

- Integrates **Keplerian orbits** and optionally full N‑body dynamics.
- Uses the **IAU‑recognised names and definitions**.
- Incorporates **dwarf planets, moons, and small bodies** from the Minor Planet Center catalogues.
- Models the **heliosphere** as a spherical boundary at 120 AU.
- Includes the **Oort cloud** as a probabilistic source of long‑period comets.

## Overview

A **gravitational singularity**, spacetime singularity, or simply singularity, is a theoretical condition in which gravity is predicted to be so intense that spacetime itself would break down catastrophically. As such, a singularity is by definition no longer part of the regular spacetime and cannot be determined by "where" or "when". Gravitational singularities exist at a junction between general relativity and quantum mechanics; therefore, the properties of the singularity cannot be described without an established theory of quantum gravity.[reference:0]

In general relativity, a singularity can be defined by the **scalar invariant curvature** becoming infinite or, more precisely, by a **geodesic** being **incomplete**. General relativity predicts that any object collapsing beyond its Schwarzschild radius would form a black hole, inside which a singularity will form. However, a black hole singularity is covered by an event horizon, so it is never in the causal past of any outside observer.[reference:1]

## Interpretation

Many theories in physics have mathematical singularities of one kind or another. Equations for these physical theories predict that the ball of mass of some quantity becomes infinite or increases without limit. This is generally a sign for a missing piece in the theory, as in the ultraviolet catastrophe, renormalization, and instability of a hydrogen atom predicted by the Larmor formula.[reference:2]

In classical field theories including special relativity but not general relativity, one can say that a solution has a singularity at a particular point in spacetime where certain physical properties become ill-defined, with spacetime serving as a background field to locate the singularity. A singularity in general relativity is more complex because spacetime itself becomes ill-defined, and the singularity is no longer part of the regular spacetime manifold. In general relativity, a singularity cannot be defined by "where" or "when".[reference:3]

Some theories, such as **loop quantum gravity**, suggest that singularities may not exist. The idea can be stated in the form that, due to quantum gravity effects, there is a minimum distance beyond which the force of gravity no longer continues to increase as the distance between the masses becomes shorter.[reference:4]

## In Black Holes

Mathematical models of black holes based on general relativity have singularities at their centers—points where the curvature of spacetime becomes infinite, and geodesics terminate within a finite proper time. However, it is unknown whether these singularities truly exist in real black holes.[reference:5]

Physicists hold various views on the existence of singularities:

| Position | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Singularities do not exist** | Their existence would make spacetime unpredictable, signaling a breakdown of general relativity and a need for quantum gravity.[reference:6] |
| **Singularities can be resolved classically** | Some believe singularities could be resolved within current physics without introducing quantum gravity.[reference:7] |
| **Some singularities remain** | Physicists including Kip Thorne and Charles Misner believe that not all singularities can be resolved, and some likely still exist despite quantum gravity effects.[reference:8] |
| **Irrelevant** | Others believe general relativity is already incomplete, and the existence of singularities doesn't matter.[reference:9] |

According to general relativity, every black hole has a singularity inside:

- For a **non-rotating (Schwarzschild) black hole**, the singularity is a **single point** of zero volume.
- For a **rotating (Kerr) black hole**, the singularity is smeared out into a **ring singularity** that lies in the plane of rotation.

All of the mass of the black hole ends up in the singularity. Since the singularity has nonzero mass in an infinitely small space, it can be thought of as having **infinite density**.[reference:10]

### Spaghettification

Observers falling into a Schwarzschild black hole cannot avoid being carried into the singularity once they cross the event horizon. As they fall further, they will be torn apart by growing tidal forces in a process sometimes referred to as **spaghettification** or the "noodle effect".[reference:11]

### BKL (Chaotic) Singularity

When realistic perturbations (such as infalling matter or radiation) are considered, the interior of a black hole is not smooth. Instead, spacetime oscillates chaotically near the singularity—a behavior described by the **Belinskii–Khalatnikov–Lifshitz (BKL) singularity** model. Any matter falling in would experience intense tidal forces rapidly changing in direction, while being compressed into an increasingly small volume.[reference:12]

### Wormholes and Closed Timelike Curves

In charged (Reissner–Nordström) or rotating (Kerr) black holes, it is theoretically possible to avoid the central singularity. Extending these solutions reveals the hypothetical possibility of exiting the black hole into a different spacetime, with the black hole acting as a **wormhole**. It also appears possible to follow **closed timelike curves** (returning to one's own past) around the Kerr singularity, leading to problems with causality like the grandfather paradox. However, quantum gravity effects or mass inflation might prevent such curves from arising.[reference:13]

## Types of Singularities

There are multiple types of singularities, each with different physical features relevant to the theories from which they emerged, such as different shapes (conical and curved). They have also been hypothesized to occur without event horizons; these are called **naked singularities**.[reference:14]

### Conical Singularities

A **conical singularity** occurs when there is a point where the limit of some diffeomorphism invariant quantity does not exist or is infinite, in which case spacetime is not smooth at the point of the limit itself. Thus, spacetime looks like a cone around this point, where the singularity is located at the tip of the cone. The metric can be finite everywhere the coordinate system is used.

Examples of conical singularities include:
- **Cosmic strings** – hypothetical 1‑dimensional topological defects in spacetime.
- The central singularity of a **Schwarzschild black hole**.[reference:15]

### Curvature Singularities

Solutions to the equations of general relativity or another theory of gravity (such as supergravity) often result in encountering points where the metric blows up to infinity. However, many of these points are completely regular, and the infinities are merely a result of using an inappropriate coordinate system at that point (a **coordinate singularity**).

To test whether there is a true singularity at a certain point, one must check whether **diffeomorphism invariant** quantities (i.e., scalars) become infinite. Such quantities are the same in every coordinate system, so these infinities will not "go away" by a change of coordinates.

**Example: Schwarzschild Solution**

In coordinate systems convenient for working far away from the black hole, a part of the metric becomes infinite at the event horizon. However, spacetime at the event horizon is regular, which becomes evident when changing to another coordinate system (such as **Kruskal coordinates**), where the metric is perfectly smooth. In the center of the black hole, where the metric becomes infinite as well, the solutions suggest a singularity exists. The existence of the singularity can be verified by noting that the **Kretschmann scalar** (the square of the Riemann tensor) becomes infinite at that point.[reference:16]

**Point vs. Ring Singularity:**
- In a non-rotating black hole, the singularity occurs at a single point in the model coordinates, called a **point singularity**.
- In a rotating (Kerr) black hole, the singularity occurs on a ring (a circular line), known as a **ring singularity**. Such a singularity may also theoretically become a wormhole.[reference:17]

**Geodesic Incompleteness:**
More generally, a spacetime is considered singular if it is **geodesically incomplete**, meaning that there are freely-falling particles whose motion cannot be determined beyond a finite time, being after the point of reaching the singularity. For example, any observer inside the event horizon of a non-rotating black hole would fall into its center within a finite period of time.[reference:18]

### Naked Singularities

Until the early 1990s, it was widely believed that general relativity hides every singularity behind an event horizon, making **naked singularities** impossible. This is referred to as the **cosmic censorship hypothesis**. However, in 1991, physicists Stuart Shapiro and Saul Teukolsky performed computer simulations of a rotating plane of dust that indicated general relativity might allow for "naked" singularities. What these objects would actually look like in such a model is unknown. However, it is hypothesized that light entering a singularity would similarly have its geodesics terminated, thus making the naked singularity look like a black hole.[reference:19]

**Disappearing Event Horizons:**

Disappearing event horizons exist in the **Kerr metric** (spinning black hole) if the angular momentum exceeds a critical value:
- For a rotating black hole, if: J > M² (in geometric units)
- For a charged (Reissner–Nordström) black hole, if: Q > M

These are known as **extremal black holes** possessing the lowest possible mass/angular-momentum/charge ratio appropriate for a given mass, angular momentum, or charge. Beyond this extremal limit, the event horizon disappears entirely, leaving a naked singularity.[reference:20]

## Regular (Nonsingular) Black Holes

To solve technical issues with general relativity, some models of gravity do not include black hole singularities. These theoretical black holes without singularities are called **regular**, or **nonsingular**, black holes.

| Model | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Fuzzball (String Theory)** | Black holes are actually made up of quantum microstates and need not have a singularity or an event horizon. |
| **Loop Quantum Gravity** | Proposes that the curvature and density at the center of a black hole is large, but not infinite. |

These models suggest that quantum gravitational effects resolve the singularity, replacing it with a region of finite, though extreme, curvature.[reference:21]

## Singularities and Quantum Gravity

The singularities predicted by classical general relativity highlight an essential limitation of the theory. At extreme densities and curvature scales (the Planck scale), quantum mechanical effects become important, and a complete theory of **quantum gravity** is thought to be necessary to describe physics in these regimes.

Leading approaches to quantum gravity that address singularities include:
- **String Theory** – via the fuzzball and other models.
- **Loop Quantum Gravity** – via quantum geometry and the elimination of singularities.
- **Causal Set Theory** – via discrete spacetime structures.
- **Asymptotically Safe Gravity** – via running gravitational couplings.

None of these approaches have yet been experimentally confirmed, but each offers a potential resolution to the problem of singularities.[reference:22][reference:23]

## Cosmological Singularities

General relativity also predicts that the initial state of the universe at the beginning of the **Big Bang** was a singularity of infinite density, infinite temperature, and infinite spacetime curvature. The classical Big Bang model contains a causal singularity at the start of time (t = 0), where all time-like geodesics have no extensions into the past. Extrapolating backward to this hypothetical time results in a universe with all spatial dimensions of size zero. However, classical gravitational theories are not expected to be accurate under these conditions, and a quantum description is likely needed.[reference:24][reference:25]

## 1. AI‑Driven Autonomous Discovery Core

### 1.1 Symbolic Regression for Field Equation Generation
A built‑in symbolic regression engine (using genetic programming and graph neural networks) continuously analyses the deviations between the simulated Einstein‑field‑equation dynamics and the *real* evolution of modified spacetimes (e.g., those with exotic matter or alternative topologies). When a consistent anomaly pattern emerges, the engine **derives candidate modified field equations** in analytical form.  
- Output: e.g., \( G_{\mu\nu} + \alpha R^2 g_{\mu\nu} + \beta C_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}C^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu} \) with fitted coefficients.  
- The system automatically cross‑checks these equations against the full validation suite (Mercury, light deflection, binary pulsars, etc.) and ranks them by agreement with known data.

### 1.2 Deep Learning Anomaly Classifier
A convolutional neural network trained on millions of pre‑computed spacetime curvature “images” (4D Riemann tensor slices) watches the simulation in real time. It classifies anomalies into categories:  
- **Wormhole formation** (Einstein‑Rosen bridge signatures)  
- **Time‑machine precursors** (closed timelike curves)  
- **Violations of the Null Energy Condition**  
- **New long‑range scalar fields** (e.g., dark energy candidates)  
When an event is flagged, the system generates a human‑readable hypothesis and saves the exact parameter snapshot for reproduction.

### 1.3 Interactive AI Co‑Scientist
A large language model fine‑tuned on physics papers (arXiv, INSPIRE) and the entire Wikipedia corpus acts as an interactive research partner.  
- **Voice/Natural‑Language Queries**: “What happens if I add a phantom scalar field with negative kinetic energy near a Kerr black hole?”  
- **The AI suggests parameter ranges**, predicts outcomes based on literature, and then **interprets the simulation results** in real time, highlighting if something genuinely new occurred.  
- It maintains a lab notebook, drafting discovery reports with references.

## 4. Speculative Physics Sandbox

This is the ultimate “what‑if” laboratory, allowing users to **break fundamental constants and symmetries** and observe the consequences.

### 4.1 Variable Constants & Dimensionality
- **Speed of light c**: make it anisotropic, time‑varying, or imaginary.  
- **Gravitational constant G**: allow it to change sign or magnitude locally.  
- **Spatiotemporal dimensions**: add extra compact or extended dimensions (up to \(5, 6, … 10+1\)D). The 4D UI extends to show slices of the full higher‑dimensional space.  
- **Signature changes**: explore transitions from Lorentzian \((- + + +)\) to Euclidean \((+ + + +)\) in certain regions.

### 4.2 Exotic Matter & Energy Conditions
- **Phantom energy** (\(w < -1\)), **ghost condensates**, **Chaplygin gas**.  
- **Negative mass** (runaway motion, gravitational dipoles) – already possible but now with full visual feedback of repulsive spacetime curvature.  
- **Imaginary mass tachyons** whose world‑lines are spacelike; the UI draws them with warning colours.

### 4.3 Custom Force Laws
A visual programming interface lets users draw **new interaction potentials** (e.g., \(V(r) \propto 1/r, e^{-r}, \sin(kr)\)) and assign them to hypothetical “fifth force” particles. The simulator then runs an N‑body simulation including that force and visualises the resulting geodesic modifications.

### 5.1 Shared Universe Environments
Multiple users connect to the same simulation in real time (similar to a multiplayer game). One user might place an exotic singularity; another watches the resulting causal disruption from a different 4D viewpoint. Collective exploration accelerates the discovery of rare phenomena.

### 5.2 Discovery Challenges & Leaderboards
Weekly challenges like “Find a stable naked singularity configuration” or “Create a traversable wormhole that lasts 5 seconds of proper time”. Successful solutions are verified by the validation suite and scored. The global leaderboard drives community innovation.

### 5.3 Open Analysis Pipelines
Users can publish their simulation configurations and the corresponding anomaly logs as **immutable datasets on a decentralised ledger**. A peer‑review system allows other users to reproduce and confirm new findings, turning the simulator into a **citizen‑science platform for fundamental physics**.
